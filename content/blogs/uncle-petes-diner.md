There are some restaurants you discover because everyone is talking about them. Others you find because they occupy a busy corner downtown or sit along a major highway. And then there are places like Uncle Pete's Diner.

On a warm Friday afternoon, a convoy of Eat the Strip regulars and first timers made the drive south of Fredericton toward Rusagonis. The city gradually faded into winding country roads, forests, and stretches of farmland. After crossing the covered bridge, small signs for the diner began appearing here and there. Not many. Just enough to reassure us that we were still heading in the right direction.

Then, almost without warning, the trees opened up. A driveway appeared. And there it was. 

The unmistakable Uncle Pete's Diner sign standing proudly in the clearing.

By the time we pulled into the parking lot, our group numbered around fifteen people, the largest turnout in Eat the Strip history. With our time in Fredericton beginning to wind down, it felt fitting that one of our final outings would bring us somewhere like this.

The moment you walk through the door, you understand why people make the drive.

The dining room is one of the coziest spaces I have encountered anywhere in New Brunswick. Historic photographs and paintings depicting local communities line the walls. Children's drawings hang proudly nearby. One colourful picture was addressed directly to Pete and the staff. Along one side of the room sit a handful of tables dressed with paper placemats covered in advertisements for local businesses.

There are booster seats tucked away for young families. High chairs waiting for the next generation of regulars. Alissa immediately pointed out how cute the children's artwork was.

Nothing about the room feels manufactured. It feels lived in. It feels loved.

You get the sense that countless Sunday afternoons have unfolded within these walls. Parents treating their children to breakfast after church. Friends gathering over coffee. ATV riders stopping in after a day on the trails. It feels less like a restaurant and more like a community gathering place where everyone is welcome.

Despite our unannounced arrival, Peter Lavigne—better known as Uncle Pete—along with his wife and staff welcomed our unusually large group with open arms. Their hospitality ranks among the best we have experienced during our years running this blog.

Within minutes it felt as though we had been invited into someone's home.

Outside, the family owns the neighbouring property where visitors will find picnic tables, a small pond, and even a miniature covered bridge. Maple trees dot the landscape, many of which the family taps themselves to produce maple syrup. Like so much else here, it is another reminder that Uncle Pete's is deeply rooted in the community around it.

Jacob and I decided to share two dishes: the fish and chips and a traditional breakfast.

The fish arrived coated in a thin, crispy batter that shattered pleasantly with each bite. Beneath the crunch, the fish remained remarkably moist and flaky. It was indulgent without being heavy, the sort of fish and chips that reminds you why the dish became a Maritime staple in the first place. A touch of seasoning elevated everything without overpowering the fish itself.

The traditional breakfast was exactly what you hope for when ordering breakfast at a rural diner. The sausage was classic diner fare, savoury and comforting. The eggs were cooked perfectly. Thick slices of toast arrived generously buttered and tasted unmistakably homemade. It was the kind of bread designed to soak up every last bit of egg yolk from the plate.

![Uncle Pete's traditional breakfast proves that simple diner fare is often the most satisfying](/uploads/img_1823.jpeg "Uncle Pete's Diner traditional breakfast")

Across the table, Alissa and Lily both ordered the club sandwich.

The first thing Alissa noticed was the cheese.

Apparently, the absence of cheese on club sandwiches at other establishments has become a recurring source of frustration for her, so she was pleased to find it included here. The bacon was crispy, the chicken was juicy and tender, and there was just enough mayonnaise to tie everything together. Lily summed it up best when she described it as a beautiful sandwich.

Gemma opted for the poutine.

The dish arrived piled high with fresh cheese curds that were both soft and satisfyingly squeaky. The rich gravy coated every fry while the curds helped hold everything together. It was hearty, comforting, and unapologetically authentic.

As impressive as the food was, it was speaking with Pete that helped explain why the restaurant feels so special.

Owning a restaurant had been a lifelong dream. Before opening his own place, he spent years cooking in other establishments. His first restaurant venture opened in the Prospect Inn in 2013, but rising rents and increasing costs eventually forced him to rethink his approach. Rather than continue paying someone else, he decided to build his own restaurant. As a carpenter by trade, Pete had the skills to make it happen.

![Fish and chips with a perfectly crisp batter and flaky, moist fish that delivers classic Maritime comfort](/uploads/img_1824.jpeg "Uncle Pete's Diner fish and chips")

The current diner opened in 2019, though its early days were shaped by unfortunate timing. Pete pointed toward hooks mounted near the ceiling and explained they were originally intended to hold curtains that would divide sections of the dining room when pandemic restrictions arrived shortly after opening.

Owning the building has allowed him to keep costs manageable in an industry where expenses continue to climb.

"I've noticed all the food is going up and down,” he said. “It's getting a little tougher."

Even so, affordability remains one of his priorities.

"I try to keep them as low as I can and still survive."

That commitment is immediately evident on the menu. Our breakfast cost just over ten dollars. Gemma's poutine hovered around ten. Even the fish and chips came in at roughly fifteen dollars. At a time when restaurant prices seem to climb monthly, the value here is remarkable.

Pete grew up on Fredericton's north side before moving to the area in 2008. Since opening the diner, support from local residents has helped sustain the business.

"We have a lot of support from the community and it means a lot to me."

While many people in Fredericton have never heard of Uncle Pete's, Pete laughed as he described how those who know about it often travel considerable distances to return. Customers regularly visit from Saint John, Harvey, and beyond.

When asked what first-time visitors should order, he did not hesitate. "Our clubs are a big hit and usually our breakfast is a big thing. Burgers are really good."

Pete hopes to keep running the diner for another five years before eventually passing it on to someone else.

"I won't shut it down completely."

Partway through our conversation, our server Emma overheard us talking and decided to offer her own perspective.

"This guy saved my life."

She spoke about how Pete and his family had supported her and how working at the diner feels less like a job and more like belonging to a family. Everything served here, she explained, feels like it is made with genuine care. When I asked about her favourite item on the menu, she immediately pointed to the French toast topped with warm blueberries and whipped cream.

Pete smiled as she walked away.

"I want people to feel like home when they come in."

Standing inside Uncle Pete's Diner, it is hard to imagine a better description.

The restaurant feels homemade because so much of it literally is. The maple syrup comes from trees on the property. The baking is scratch-made. The coconut cream pie disappears almost as quickly as it appears. The building itself was constructed by the owner.

Everything about this place reflects the people behind it.

In a region filled with hidden gems, Uncle Pete's may be one of the most hidden of all. Yet for those willing to make the drive down the country roads toward Rusagonis, they will discover one of the warmest dining rooms in New Brunswick, food that tastes like it came from a family kitchen, and hospitality that is increasingly difficult to find.

Just make sure to check their hours before heading out. Like many rural establishments, they keep specific operating times, with updates often posted on Facebook.

Some restaurants serve a meal. Others make you feel at home. Uncle Pete's somehow manages to do both.
