Fredericton has a funny way of hiding some of its best food. Sometimes it’s tucked beside a gas station, behind an unassuming strip mall façade, or buried in a part of town you only end up in for one oddly specific errand. Country Fried might be one of the more literal examples of this.

On a sunny Thursday afternoon, Joel, Lily and I made our way to the Fredericton Exhibition Grounds on Smythe Street in search of a fabled poutine. Ryan, the owner of Country Fried, had messaged me nearly a year ago shortly after I moved to Fredericton, proudly brandishing his credentials as winner of Community Votes Fredericton’s best poutine in both 2023 and 2024 and insisting I needed to come try it for myself. Better late than never.

Actually finding the restaurant turned out to be part of the experience.

Country Fried now operates out of a permanent indoor location inside the Exhibition Grounds after moving from the outdoor food stalls in December 2025. On the day we visited, however, the neighbouring Elm City Quilters Guild Anniversary Quilt Show had effectively transformed the building into a maze of colour and fabric. We accidentally entered through the quilt exhibition first, wandering through rows upon rows of stitched landscapes, floral patterns and hanging blankets before somehow emerging at Country Fried hidden behind the labyrinth like a secret level in a video game.

The restaurant itself is modest and comfortable, with around a dozen tables, black-painted walls and a fryer basket logo painted proudly near the counter. Awards line the walls, subtle reminders that this tiny operation has quietly built a reputation around town.

Two men greeted us warmly from behind the counter as soon as we walked in, immediately making us feel comfortable. One of them was Jet, a cook who has been working with Ryan for about half a year. After our meal he came over to chat, talking about the challenges and small victories of running a business hidden inside the Exhibition Grounds.

As a bit of a poutine purist, I ordered the classic. The first surprise was the price. A small poutine came in at under seven dollars, which in 2026 feels almost suspiciously affordable. Joel and Lily both opted for the buffalo chicken poutine.

Less than ten minutes later, the food arrived piping hot.

The fries immediately stood out. Thin-cut, crispy and still carrying bits of skin, they were exactly what you want anchoring a proper poutine. There was crunch underneath the gravy instead of instant sogginess, and the ratios felt carefully balanced. Most importantly, there were actual cheese curds here. Not shredded mozzarella masquerading as poutine cheese. Real curds with that familiar squeak. 

![A straightforward, no-nonsense poutine with crispy skin-on fries, proper cheese curds and rich gravy done exactly right](/uploads/img_1625.jpeg "Country Fried classic poutine")

The unanimous verdict around the table was that this place absolutely delivers.

Lily pointed directly to the fries as the star of the show. Joel loved the way the buffalo sauce interacted with the gravy, noting the sweetness from the buffalo sauce mixing with the saltiness of both the gravy and chicken in a way that somehow worked better than expected. The chicken itself carried a nice heat without overpowering everything else.

Jet later told us the buffalo chicken poutine and chicken bacon ranch poutine are among their hotter sellers. He also recommended the pulled pork poutine and mentioned that for customers wanting something lighter, the wontons and sandwiches have started building a following too.

Country Fried’s story has been one of constant adaptation. The business originally started on Fredericton’s north side under previous ownership focused solely on poutines before Ryan took things over and expanded the menu. Since moving into the Exhibition Grounds, they’ve had to navigate both the opportunities and unpredictability that come with the venue. Wrestling nights can generate enough business to cover an entire month’s rent, Jet told us, while other events can accidentally choke off traffic entirely if vendors or displays block their visibility. On one recent day, he said, they had only a single customer because the front façade was completely obstructed.

Even finding the entrance remains a challenge. Jet laughed while explaining how many people still don’t know where they are despite signage outside. Hidden gem gets thrown around a lot these days, but in this case it feels genuinely earned.

Still, there’s optimism. Ryan is trying to keep prices down by buying ingredients in bulk, something increasingly rare as food costs continue climbing across the province. Jet mentioned they hope to continue expanding the menu and stay in the Exhibition Grounds long term, assuming future redevelopment plans for the property don’t eventually force another move. If that happens, he said, they’d likely pivot toward a food truck model.

For now though, Country Fried feels like one of those deeply Fredericton places: humble, a little difficult to locate, staffed by genuinely kind people and serving food that far exceeds what the setting initially suggests.

If you’re craving a classic poutine that doesn’t destroy your wallet, this is absolutely worth the trip. Between the crispy skin-on fries, proper cheese curds and warm service from the lads behind the counter, it’s easy to understand why Country Fried has picked up accolades around town. Don’t let the unusual location deter you. Hidden behind quilts and exhibition halls is some seriously solid poutine, and after this visit, we’re already planning a return trip for the sandwiches and wontons.
